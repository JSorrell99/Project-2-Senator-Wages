Minimum wage freeze

Senators prosper, minimum wage workers struggle: A century of wage data

By Janean Sorrell

The average U.S. Senator earns more than 11 times the annual income of a minimum wage worker, and thanks to inflation, the wage gap is growing.

Over the last 100 years, U.S. Senators' salaries have seen substantial increases, far outpacing the growth of the federal minimum wage. As depicted in the first chart, senators' annual salaries have risen sharply since the early 20th century, reaching $174,000 in 2024. In contrast, the minimum wage, currently at $7.25 per hour, equates to an annual salary of just $15,080 for a full-time worker— a figure that has remained unchanged since 2009.

In stark contrast, U.S. Senators receive a base annual salary of $174,000. This figure does not include additional compensation that may come from roles such as committee chairs or leadership positions, which can further boost their earnings. Senators also benefit from a comprehensive benefits package, including health insurance, retirement plans, and allowances for travel and staff.

  • Federal minimum wage was enacted in 1938 at $0.25 per hour.
  • The last increase was in 2009, marking the longest gap without any change.

Adjusting for inflation reveals an even more pronounced disparity. The second chart shows inflation-adjusted salaries for both U.S. Senators and minimum wage workers. Although senators' salaries have experienced fluctuations, they remain significantly higher than inflation-adjusted minimum wage earnings. The adjusted minimum wage barely rises above $15,080, struggling to keep up with the cost of living over the decades.

The federal minimum wage of $7.25 per hour is just that—a minimum. Many states and municipalities have implemented higher minimum wages to better reflect the cost of living in their regions. For instance, California’s minimum wage is set at $15.50 per hour as of January, and Washington, D.C., boasts an even higher rate of $16.50 per hour. Despite these regional adjustments, the federal rate remains unchanged, leaving workers in many parts of the country struggling to make ends meet.

  • Five states: Alabama, Louisiana, Mississippi, South Carolina and Tennessee — do not have a state minimum wage, defaulting to the federal rate of $7.25 per hour.
  • Annual Adjustments: Some states adjust their minimum wage rates annually based on inflation or cost-of-living indices. For example, Colorado's minimum wage is adjusted based on the Consumer Price Index.
  • Minimum Wage and Youth: A handful of states have different minimum wage rates for minors or younger workers, often set lower than the standard rate. In Georgia, minors under 18 can earn a lower minimum wage, which is often less than the standard adult rate.
  • Tipped Minimum Wage: Many states have a different minimum wage for tipped employees, which is generally lower than the standard minimum wage. This rate is supplemented by the total tips received to meet the minimum wage requirement.

This growing wage gap underscores the broader economic inequalities in the U.S. According to the Economic Policy Institute, the top 1% of earners hold more wealth than the bottom 90% combined. This concentration of wealth has fueled debates over the need for more equitable distribution of resources and has heightened calls for increasing the federal minimum wage.

Advocates for raising the minimum wage argue that it would help reduce poverty, boost consumer spending, and lessen dependence on government assistance programs. Critics, however, warn that higher wages could lead to job losses as businesses may cut positions or reduce hours to offset increased labor costs. They also caution that rising wages could drive up prices for goods and services, potentially negating the benefits for low-wage workers.

Efforts to raise the federal minimum wage have faced significant political challenges. The Raise the Wage Act of 2021, which proposed gradually increasing the federal minimum wage to $15 per hour by 2025, passed the House of Representatives but stalled in the Senate. In the absence of federal action, states and municipalities continue to set their own wage standards.

The discrepancy between minimum wage earnings and senators' salaries highlights the ongoing struggles faced by low-wage workers in the U.S. While senators earn a comfortable income with comprehensive benefits, many minimum wage workers live paycheck to paycheck, unable to afford basic necessities.

Methodology

This analysis uses data from the U.S. Department of Labor, Senate.gov, and the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis. Historical wage data was adjusted for inflation using the Consumer Price Index (CPI) via a Python package. Visualizations were created with Datawrapper. All data is current as of August 2024.

For state minimum wage analysis, I used the general minimum wage applicable to most workers. Note that some localities may have higher minimum wages not reflected in this state-level analysis.

Detailed information on data sources, processing methods, and visualization techniques can be found in the project's readme file: https://github.com/JSorrell99/Project-2-Senator-Wages
